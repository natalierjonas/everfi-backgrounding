§ 01 Background & Business Model Sources: fn:7,9,13,14,18

EverFi pioneered a model it called "Impact-as-a-Service": corporations — mainly banks, credit unions, and insurance companies — pay EverFi to place ostensibly free digital curriculum inside K-12 classrooms. Students receive financial literacy lessons; banks receive branded content delivery, community reinvestment credit, and access to students and families as a future customer base.

Founded in 2008 by Tom Davidson and Jon Chapman in a Georgetown apartment, EverFi grew from a single financial literacy product into a multi-vertical education technology platform serving three distinct markets: K-12 schools (bank-sponsored financial literacy), higher education (mandatory compliance training for alcohol awareness and sexual assault prevention), and corporate clients (workplace ethics and compliance training).

"Financial institutions typically pay EverFi for the software, and then underwrite its offering in public schools. It's good advertising and PR for banks, credit unions, and insurance companies that sponsor these programs." — The American Prospect, "As Consumer Protections Dwindle, Schools Push Financial Literacy" [fn:13]

By the time Blackbaud acquired the company in 2022, EverFi claimed to have reached over 45 million learners globally. Its K-12 programs operated in more than 21,000 schools and 7,000 school districts. The sponsorship partners included Capital One (branding its curriculum "Modern Money"[fn:18]), SouthEast Bank, Edward Jones, MassMutual, and dozens of regional banks and credit unions.[fn:14]

The higher education division — which included AlcoholEdu (the largest online course in higher education, per the company) and Haven (sexual assault prevention, used at more than 400 institutions) — was carved out and sold to Vector Solutions in July 2021 for $100 million before the Blackbaud deal closed.[fn:17] These programs were sold to universities as Title IX and Clery Act compliance tools; critics questioned whether online completion rates translated into behavioral outcomes.[fn:15]

§ 02 Corporate Structure & State Registrations Sources: fn:1,2,5,20–23,26

§ 03 Financial History & Value Destruction Sources: fn:7,8,9,10,16,17,19
EverFi Valuation Timeline — Milestones vs. Blackbaud Era (2010–2024)

EverFi raised $251 million or more across multiple venture rounds, drawing in a roster of Silicon Valley–adjacent investors whose imprimatur validated the company's social impact framing. Jeff Bezos invested personally through Bezos Expeditions. Google's then-executive chairman Eric Schmidt invested through Tomorrow Ventures. Twitter co-founder Evan Williams, U2 singer Bono, TPG's The Rise Fund, and New Enterprise Associates all participated.[fn:10,16]

The July 2021 sale of its higher education compliance division to Florida-based Vector Solutions for $100 million was a prelude to the main event. Six months later, Blackbaud acquired the remaining EverFi business — primarily its K-12 and corporate verticals — for approximately $750 million: $450 million in cash and 3,844,423 shares of Blackbaud common stock then valued at roughly $300 million.[fn:7] At the time, EverFi was projected to add $120 million in 2022 revenue growing at roughly 20% annually.

The combined entity would "double Blackbaud's total addressable market to over $20 billion." EverFi's founder Tom Davidson joined Blackbaud to lead "corporate sector expansion." — Blackbaud press release, January 3, 2022 [fn:7]

The growth did not materialize. Under Blackbaud's ownership, the EVERFI segment saw a 26% revenue decline. Blackbaud recorded a noncash impairment charge of approximately $415 million against the EVERFI asset group — nearly equal to the entire cash purchase price paid two years earlier. On December 31, 2024, Blackbaud sold EVERFI to an unnamed private investment firm for a "nominal amount." No specific sale price was disclosed.[fn:8,12,19]

A key driver of the higher education compliance business was federal pressure on universities under Title IX and the Clery Act. When the Obama administration's "Dear Colleague" letter in 2011 heightened Title IX enforcement, universities scrambled for documented compliance tools. EverFi's Haven program benefited from this regulatory environment, partnering with the White House "It's On Us" campaign.[fn:11] The Trump administration's subsequent loosening of Title IX guidance contributed to reduced urgency — and may have weakened demand in the university market.

§ 06 Risk Flags & Analytical Notes Sources: fn:6,8,13,15,19
Value destruction
HIGH
Bought for $750M in 2022; sold for nominal amount in 2024. $415M impairment charge recorded. Raises questions about structural durability of the sponsorship-funded curriculum model at scale. Enigma Negative News returned a "low" risk overall, but this predates the divestiture announcement.[fn:8,19]
Revenue model conflict
MED
Banks and financial institutions sponsoring school financial literacy curricula with their own branding creates a structural tension: the educational content serves dual masters. No independent curriculum efficacy studies are consistently funded by the major sponsors.[fn:13]
Compliance training effectiveness
MED
University sexual assault prevention training (Haven) and alcohol awareness (AlcoholEdu) have been challenged by researchers who question whether online completion translates to behavior change, or serves primarily as institutional liability management.[fn:15]
Student data / COPPA/FERPA
MED
Operating in K-12 classrooms at scale creates inherent COPPA and FERPA compliance obligations. Enigma Negative News flagged this as a structural forward risk even in the absence of documented violations.[fn:6]
Parent company breach
MED
Blackbaud (EverFi's 2022–2024 parent) suffered a major ransomware breach in 2020 affecting nonprofit and education data. EverFi was not directly implicated but shared organizational infrastructure during the Blackbaud period.
Organizational instability
MED
Employee reviews during the Blackbaud era cited management turnover, reorganizations, and cultural stress. Multiple revoked state registrations (DC, VA, GA, IL, IN) suggest active administrative wind-down rather than steady state operations.[fn:1,6]
Unknown buyer identity
MED
As of this retrieval date, Blackbaud has not publicly disclosed the buyer's identity. The "nominal" sale price and undisclosed acquirer make it difficult to assess EverFi's current operational and financial health.[fn:8]
